The Feet Were the Case

The initiating signal was not incidental. It was the problem the system accepted.

PRIMARY SIGNAL

The case did not begin with an abstract diagnosis. It began with feet that could not be made to behave like ordinary feet.

The feet were not a side issue. They were the first signal, the persistent signal, the displaced signal, and the signal that later made the record impossible to defend without diminishing its own language.

This matters because institutions often protect themselves by changing the level of description. A person enters with a concrete bodily problem: pain, posture, gait, work failure, inability to move normally through the world. The system translates that into a diagnostic abstraction. Sometimes abstraction is necessary. Medicine cannot function without it. But abstraction becomes dangerous when it stops being answerable to the signal that created the duty in the first place.

In this case, the signal was foot-centred. The patient came into the medical system with gait failure in the context of significant foot pathology and abnormal movement. The feet were not merely decorative. They were not late noise. They were not a complaint added after the fact. They were the problem around which the referral formed.

If the feet were peripheral, why did they remain the central complaint for years? If they were central, why were they sent out of neurology?

That question is not rhetorical decoration. It is the structural key. Once the hospital accepted the referral, it accepted responsibility for explaining a foot-centred gait failure. It could explain the feet neurologically. It could falsify them as causative. It could supersede them by showing that a better explanation had fully absorbed the presentation. But it could not simply allow the feet to remain vivid in the record while losing authority over the diagnostic frame.

That is what happened. The feet stayed present, but they were demoted. They became a parallel problem. A podiatry problem. A pain problem. A surface problem. A complication. A magnified symptom. A thing discussed around the edges of a Parkinsonian premise rather than the thing capable of interrogating that premise.

This is why the record is so powerful. It does not show clean neglect. It shows something more revealing. The feet were repeatedly encountered but never restored to governing status. They were visible without being authoritative.

A fact can be visible and still denied governing force.

The distinction matters. If the feet had been absent from the documents, the hospital might say the patient was reconstructing the case through memory. But the documents do not permit that easy defence. The feet are there. The pain is there. The abnormal gait is there. The footdrop-like appearance is there. The dystonic posture is there. The referral pathway is there. Later medication response is there. The difficulty is not proving that the feet existed. The difficulty is explaining why they were never allowed to control the question.

This is the central public lesson: records can harm not only by omitting facts, but by subordinating the right facts. A record can be detailed and still be misleading if the wrong facts are allowed to govern.

The problem is not that the record was silent. The problem is that it spoke against itself.

The feet also expose the weakness of treating this as a simple disagreement about diagnosis. The patient does not need to prove that every clinician should instantly have understood the final explanation. That is not the point. The point is that the original foot-centred problem remained unresolved at the very moment the system moved toward finality.

A diagnosis can be reasonable as a hypothesis and still unsafe as an endpoint. Parkinsonism may have been a reasonable direction. It may have been clinically understandable. It may even have captured part of the truth. But it did not discharge the institution's duty to answer the initiating problem unless it explained why the feet behaved as they did.

Reasonableness of suspicion is not reasonableness of closure.

The later medication story makes the displacement harder to defend. If Parkinson's medication relieved the pain, then the pain was dopaminergically material. If the pain was dopaminergically material, it could not safely be treated as a merely peripheral nuisance. If the institution says the medication improved the experience of pain without explaining its cause, then the causal question remains open. Magnification is not diagnosis. Relief is not irrelevant.

That is why the old explanatory refuge becomes unstable. If alcohol, neuropathy, podiatry, or surface pathology explained the feet, then the record must explain why central medication repeatedly affected the same problem. If Parkinsonian medication explained the response, then the record must explain why the original foot complaint was treated as separate.

If the pain was not Parkinson's, why did Parkinson's medication relieve it? If it was Parkinson's, why was the original foot complaint treated as separate?

This is not an argument that every pain response proves a diagnosis. It is more careful than that. The argument is that a major medication response to the original governing symptom made the symptom diagnostically material. It required reclassification, or at least explicit reconciliation. It could not be safely absorbed as anecdote while the old frame remained untouched.

The feet are also where the human and administrative parts of the case meet. Administratively, they define the accepted scope. Clinically, they carry the unresolved signal. Narratively, they are where the patient lived the injury. The case is powerful because these are not separate stories. The same bodily fact runs through them all.

This is why the public archive must keep returning to the feet. Not for emotional emphasis. For precision. The feet are the control variable. If the hospital says the diagnosis was settled, the feet ask when the initiating problem was resolved. If the hospital says the case remained open, the feet ask why finality proceeded. If the hospital says care continued, the feet ask whether care ever made the original premise vulnerable.

If reviewed, why not revised? If discussed, why not resolved? If recorded, why not allowed to govern?

The truth is that the feet became institutionally inconvenient. They were too central to ignore completely, but too disruptive to permit as the organising fact. So the record did something subtler. It kept them. It described them. It moved them. It managed them. But it did not let them decide anything.

That is why the phrase "the feet were the case" is not metaphor. It is a statement of sequence. They initiated the referral. They persisted through the record. They exposed the incompleteness of closure. They later became pharmacologically revealing. And they remain the simplest way for a public reader to understand what went wrong.

CLOSING FINDING

The hospital did not have to know everything in 2017. It did have to remain answerable to the problem it accepted.

The feet were not incidental to the case.

They were the case.
